More information: how this ET chart was generated

This chart shows forecast reference evapotranspiration, often written as ET₀, for the representative sites you select. ET₀ is an estimate of how much water the atmosphere could pull from a well-watered reference crop under expected weather conditions. It is driven by temperature, wind, humidity, and solar radiation, so hotter, windier, and drier places usually plot higher than cooler or more humid places.

The site traces are based on forecast weather data and are intended as a simple planning signal, not a direct measurement of crop use or irrigation applied. A value of 0.20 inches in a day means the atmosphere is demanding about one-fifth of an inch of water from the reference surface that day. Overlaying sites helps viewers see why western Nebraska often needs more irrigation water per acre than central Nebraska over the same period.

Data notes

Reservoir charts use the uploaded workbook and current public readings where available.
  • Lake McConaughy teacup fill uses the current CNPPID percent-capacity value (51.1%). The other Nebraska teacup fills are now storage-based, using current storage divided by each reservoir's historical maximum storage from the workbook so the teacup view is consistent with the reservoir condition statistics.
  • Jeffrey, Johnson, and Elwood current elevations and storage are updated from the same CNPPID page; Maloney and Sutherland continue to use the latest workbook observation date because no comparable live public current reading was available in the CNPPID table.
  • Reservoir probability bands exclude the first five years of each record to reduce initial-fill distortion.
  • The short-term outlook section is a screening summary derived from the dashboard’s current snowpack, stream gage, drought, and PRRIP context so users can quickly interpret likely runoff and flow pressure over the next 7 to 14 days.
